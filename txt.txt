<b>Mayday</b> is an emergency <a href="/wiki/Procedure_word" title="Procedure word">procedure word</a> used internationally as a <a href="/wiki/Distress_signal" title="Distress signal">distress signal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Voice_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Voice procedure">voice-procedure</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio communications</a>.
</p><p>It is used to signal a life-threatening emergency primarily by aviators and mariners, but in some countries local organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Firefighters" class="mw-redirect" title="Firefighters">firefighters</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> forces, and transportation organizations also use the term. Convention requires the word be repeated three times in a row during the initial emergency declaration ("Mayday mayday mayday").
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The "mayday" procedure word was conceived as a distress call in the early 1920s by Frederick Stanley Mockford, officer-in-charge of radio at <a href="/wiki/Croydon_Airport" title="Croydon Airport">Croydon Airport</a>, England. He had been asked to think of a word that would indicate distress and would easily be understood by all pilots and ground staff in an emergency.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since much of the air traffic at the time was between Croydon and <a href="/wiki/Le_Bourget_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Bourget Airport">Le Bourget Airport</a> in Paris, he proposed the term "mayday", the phonetic equivalent of the French <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">m'aidez</i></span> ("help me") or <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">m'aider</i></span> (a short form of <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">venez m'aider</i></span>, "come [and] help me").<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> (M'aidez is non-standard French; the phrase <i>Aidez moi</i> is standard.) The term is unrelated to the holiday <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>.
</p><p>Following tests, the new procedure word was introduced for <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">cross-Channel</a> flights in February 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-times1923_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times1923-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The previous distress call had been the <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a> signal <a href="/wiki/SOS" title="SOS">SOS</a>, but this was not considered suitable for voice communication, "[o]wing to the difficulty of distinguishing the letter 'S' by telephone".<sup id="cite_ref-times1923_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times1923-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1927, the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Radiotelegraph Convention</a> of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> adopted the voice call "mayday" as the radiotelephone distress call in addition to the SOS radiotelegraph (Morse code) signal.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p>If a mayday call cannot be sent because a radio is not available, a variety of other <a href="/wiki/Distress_signal" title="Distress signal">distress signals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Call_for_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Call for help">calls for help</a> can be used. Additionally, a mayday call can be sent on behalf of one vessel by another; this is known as a mayday relay.
</p><p>Civilian aircraft making a mayday call in United States airspace are encouraged by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> to use the following format, omitting any portions as necessary for expediency or where they are irrelevant (capitalization as in the original source):
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Mayday, Mayday, Mayday; (Name of station addressed); Aircraft call sign and type; Nature of emergency; Weather; Pilot's intentions and/or requests; Present position and heading, or if lost then last known position and heading and time when aircraft was at that position; <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">Altitude</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flight_level" title="Flight level">Flight level</a>; Fuel remaining in minutes; Number of people on board; Any other useful information.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Making a false distress call is a criminal offense in many countries, punishable by a fine, restitution, and possible imprisonment.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Other_urgent_calls">Other urgent calls</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other urgent calls"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Pan-pan">Pan-pan</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pan-pan"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pan-pan" title="Pan-pan">Pan-pan</a></div>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Pan-pan" title="Pan-pan">Pan-pan</a>" (from the French: <i><span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">panne</i></span></i>, 'a breakdown') indicates an urgent situation, such as a mechanical failure or a medical problem, of a lower order than a "grave and imminent threat requiring immediate assistance". The suffix "medico" originally was to be added by vessels in British waters to indicate a medical problem ("pan-pan medico", repeated three times), or by aircraft declaring a non-life-threatening medical emergency of a passenger in flight, or those operating as protected medical transport in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">Geneva Conventions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> "Pan-pan medico" is no longer in official use.<sup id="cite_ref-Tim_Bartlett_2009_53_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tim_Bartlett_2009_53-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Declaring_emergency">Declaring emergency</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Declaring emergency"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Sometimes the phrase "declaring emergency" is used in aviation, as an alternative to calling "mayday".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For example, in 1998 <a href="/wiki/Swissair_Flight_111" title="Swissair Flight 111">Swissair Flight 111</a> radioed "Swissair one-eleven <a href="/wiki/Heavy_(aviation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy (aviation)">heavy</a> is declaring emergency" after their situation had worsened, upgrading from the "pan-pan" which was declared earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>However, the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">International Civil Aviation Organization</a> recommends the use of the standard "pan-pan" and "mayday" calls instead of "declaring an emergency".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Cases of pilots using phrases other than "pan-pan" and "mayday" have caused confusion and errors in aircraft handling.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Silencing_other_communications_traffic">Silencing other communications traffic</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Silencing other communications traffic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Radio_silence" title="Radio silence">Radio silence</a></div>
<p>"Seelonce mayday" (using an approximation of the French pronunciation of <i><span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">silence</i></span></i>) is a demand that the channel only be used by the vessel/s and authorities involved with the distress. The channel may not be used for normal working traffic until "seelonce feenee" is broadcast. "Seelonce mayday" and "seelonce feenee" may only be sent by the controlling station in charge of the distress. The expression "stop transmitting – mayday" is an aeronautical equivalent of "seelonce mayday". "Seelonce distress" and "prudonce" are no longer in use since ITU WRC-07.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The format for a "seelonce mayday" is MAYDAY, All Stations x3 or [Interfering station] x3, this is [controlling station], SEELONCE MAYDAY.<sup id="cite_ref-ITU-RR-2016_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITU-RR-2016-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>"Seelonce feenee" (from French <i><span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">silence fini</i></span></i>, 'silence finished') means that the emergency situation has been concluded and the channel may now be used normally. "Distress traffic ended" is the aeronautical equivalent of "seelonce feenee".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The format for the "seelonce feenee" is MAYDAY, All stations x3, this is [controlling station] x3, date and time in UTC, distressed vessels MMSI number, distressed vessels name, distressed vessels call sign, SEELONCE FEENEE.<sup id="cite_ref-ITU-RR-2016_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITU-RR-2016-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
